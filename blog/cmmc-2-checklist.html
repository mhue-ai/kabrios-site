CMMC 2.0 requirements checklist for defense contractors

March 16, 2026 · 11 min read

If you're in the defense industrial base, CMMC is no longer theoretical. The final rule went into effect in late 2024, phased implementation is underway, and DoD contracts are starting to include CMMC requirements. Here's what you need to know and what to do about it.

CMMC 2.0 in 60 seconds

The Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) 2.0 is a DoD program that verifies defense contractors and subcontractors meet specific cybersecurity standards before handling Federal Contract Information (FCI) or Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI). It replaced the self-attestation model under DFARS 252.204-7012 with tiered certification requirements.

CMMC 2.0 has three levels. Level 1 (Foundational) covers basic cyber hygiene with 17 practices from FAR 52.204-21. Level 2 (Advanced) maps to the 110 security requirements in NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2 — this is where most contractors handling CUI will land. Level 3 (Expert) adds 24 additional requirements from NIST 800-172 for the most sensitive programs.

Level 1: foundational (self-assessment)

Level 1 applies to contractors handling only FCI (not CUI). It requires 17 basic practices — things like limiting access to authorized users, sanitizing media before disposal, and using antivirus software. Assessment is through annual self-assessment with affirmation by a senior official.

Key practices: Access control (limit system access to authorized users and transactions), identification and authentication (verify identity before granting access), media protection (sanitize or destroy media before disposal), physical protection (limit physical access), system and communications protection (monitor and control communications at system boundaries), and system and information integrity (identify and manage information system flaws).

If you're a small subcontractor who only handles FCI, Level 1 is your target. It's the most straightforward path — no third-party assessment needed.

Level 2: advanced (third-party assessment for most)

Level 2 is where the real work begins. It requires implementation of all 110 security requirements in NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2, organized across 14 control families. For contracts involving prioritized CUI (the majority of CUI-handling contracts), a certified third-party assessment organization (C3PAO) must perform the assessment. For non-prioritized CUI, self-assessment with affirmation is sufficient.

The 14 control families: Access Control (22 requirements), Awareness and Training (3), Audit and Accountability (9), Configuration Management (9), Identification and Authentication (11), Incident Response (3), Maintenance (6), Media Protection (9), Personnel Security (2), Physical Protection (6), Risk Assessment (3), Security Assessment (4), System and Communications Protection (16), System and Information Integrity (7).

The SPRS score: You must calculate and submit a Supplier Performance Risk System (SPRS) score reflecting your current compliance posture. A perfect score is 110. Each unimplemented requirement reduces your score by 1, 3, or 5 points depending on its weight. A POA&M can be used for requirements not yet fully implemented, but there are limits — some requirements cannot be on a POA&M.

Level 3: expert (government-led assessment)

Level 3 adds 24 enhanced security requirements from NIST SP 800-172 on top of the full Level 2 requirements. Assessment is led by the Defense Contract Management Agency (DCMA) Defense Industrial Base Cybersecurity Assessment Center (DIBCAC). This level is reserved for the most sensitive programs involving critical CUI.

If you're at Level 3, you already know it — your contracting officer will specify the requirement. The additional controls focus on advanced threat detection, incident response, and resilience measures designed to counter nation-state-level threats.

Implementation timeline

CMMC requirements are being phased into DoD contracts over a four-phase rollout. Phase 1 (2025) includes Level 1 self-assessments and some Level 2 self-assessments in new contracts. Phase 2 adds Level 2 C3PAO assessments. Phase 3 adds Level 3. Phase 4 is full implementation across all applicable contracts.

The practical implication: if you're not already working on CMMC compliance, you're behind. Contracts are already including CMMC requirements as a condition of award. Waiting until a contract explicitly requires it means you're competing against contractors who are already certified.

Your CMMC 2.0 readiness checklist

Scope your CUI environment. Identify exactly where CUI flows in your organization — systems, networks, people, and processes. The narrower your CUI boundary, the fewer controls you need to implement and the faster your assessment goes.

Gap assess against NIST 800-171. Conduct a thorough gap assessment comparing your current security controls to the 110 requirements. Be honest — inflated SPRS scores that don't survive a C3PAO assessment create real problems.

Build your SSP and POA&M. Document your System Security Plan describing how each requirement is implemented. For any gaps, create a Plan of Action and Milestones with realistic timelines and owners.

Implement technical controls. MFA everywhere. Encryption at rest and in transit. Audit logging with tamper-evident storage. Network segmentation around your CUI boundary. Endpoint detection and response. Regular vulnerability scanning. These are non-negotiable.

Train your people. CMMC requires role-based security training. Everyone who touches CUI needs to understand their responsibilities. This includes subcontractors and partners with CUI access.

Prepare for assessment. For Level 2 with C3PAO assessment, select a certified assessor, prepare your evidence packages, and conduct an internal readiness review. The assessment will examine every control — documentation, implementation, and effectiveness.

Maintain continuous compliance. CMMC isn't a one-time exercise. You must maintain your security posture and be ready for re-assessment. Annual affirmations are required, and a lapse in compliance can affect contract eligibility.
