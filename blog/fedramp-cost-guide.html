What FedRAMP authorization actually costs in 2026

March 16, 2026 · 9 min read

FedRAMP authorization unlocks the federal market — a $100B+ cloud spend opportunity. But the path to authorization is expensive, time-consuming, and poorly understood. Here's what it actually costs, where the money goes, and how 20x is changing the equation.

The headline numbers

FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate authorization — the most common level for SaaS companies selling to federal agencies — typically costs between $250,000 and $750,000 for the initial authorization. FedRAMP High can exceed $1 million. FedRAMP Low (and the new 20x Low pilot) is significantly cheaper but still a major investment.

These numbers include everything: documentation, consulting, tooling, 3PAO assessment, remediation, and internal staff time. But the initial authorization is only part of the cost — continuous monitoring adds $100,000-$300,000 per year in ongoing operational expenses.

Where the money goes

System Security Plan (SSP) and documentation: $50,000-$150,000. The SSP is the cornerstone of your FedRAMP package. It documents every control implementation across all NIST 800-53 controls in your baseline. For Moderate, that's roughly 325 controls. Most companies hire a consulting firm to help draft this — doing it entirely in-house is possible but rare for first-timers.

3PAO assessment: $100,000-$250,000. A Third Party Assessment Organization (3PAO) performs an independent assessment of your system. This includes security testing, control validation, and the production of a Security Assessment Report (SAR). The 3PAO must be accredited by A2LA, and pricing varies significantly by firm, scope, and complexity.

Remediation: $50,000-$200,000. The 3PAO will almost certainly find issues. The Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) tracks these findings through remediation. Depending on the severity and volume of findings, remediation can be the most unpredictable cost center.

Tooling and infrastructure: $30,000-$100,000/year. You'll need tools for vulnerability scanning, log management, continuous monitoring, configuration management, and evidence collection. Some organizations use existing tools; others need to add FedRAMP-specific capabilities. AWS GovCloud or Azure Government Cloud hosting adds premium costs over commercial cloud.

Consulting support: $75,000-$200,000. Most first-time FedRAMP applicants hire a consultant or advisory firm to guide the process. Good consultants accelerate the timeline significantly and help avoid costly mistakes. Budget for 6-12 months of advisory support.

Internal staff time: often the hidden cost. FedRAMP authorization is a full-time effort for 1-3 people over 6-18 months. Even with consulting support, your internal team will spend significant time on documentation, evidence collection, architecture decisions, and remediation. The opportunity cost of diverting your security team to FedRAMP work is rarely budgeted but always felt.

FedRAMP 20x: the new math

FedRAMP 20x is the most significant change to the program since its inception. The Phase One pilot, launched in late 2025, introduced an automation-first approach for Low-impact systems. Instead of the traditional paperwork-heavy process, 20x uses key security indicators (KSIs) and automated evidence to dramatically reduce the cost and timeline.

Early 20x Low authorizations are expected to cost a fraction of traditional Rev 5 Low — potentially in the $25,000-$75,000 range for organizations that already have strong security posture. The Phase Two pilot extending to Moderate systems started in 2026, though formal pricing benchmarks for 20x Moderate won't be established until the path opens widely in late 2026.

The catch: 20x requires machine-readable OSCAL (Open Security Controls Assessment Language) authorization packages. RFC-0024 mandates all providers transition to OSCAL by September 2026. This is a technical lift that most organizations will need tooling support for.

GovRamp: the state and local parallel

GovRamp (formerly StateRAMP) applies the FedRAMP model to state, local, tribal, and educational (SLTT) governments. It's built on the same NIST 800-53 foundation and offers Moderate and Low levels. If you're already pursuing FedRAMP, GovRamp Fast Track can expedite your SLTT authorization.

GovRamp costs are generally lower than FedRAMP because the program is administered by a nonprofit and the assessment process is somewhat lighter. Budget $50,000-$150,000 for initial GovRamp verification, depending on level and existing posture.

How to reduce costs without cutting corners

Start with your existing controls. You're not building from zero. If you already have SOC 2 or ISO 27001, a significant portion of your controls map directly to NIST 800-53. The gap is smaller than you think.

Automate evidence collection. The single biggest recurring cost in FedRAMP is evidence gathering for continuous monitoring. Automating this through cloud-native tools (Security Hub, GuardDuty, Config) and a compliance platform like Kabrios can reduce the ongoing labor cost by 50-70%.

Use the 20x path if eligible. If your system qualifies for Low impact and you have strong automation, 20x offers a dramatically cheaper path to authorization.

Budget for continuous monitoring from day one. Too many organizations blow their budget on initial authorization and then struggle with the ongoing costs. ConMon is not optional — budget $100,000-$300,000/year for Moderate and plan for it from the start.
