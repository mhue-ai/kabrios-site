SOC 2 vs ISO 27001: which do you actually need?

March 16, 2026 · 8 min read

If you sell software to businesses, someone has already asked you about SOC 2 or ISO 27001. Probably both. The question isn't whether you need a compliance framework — it's which one actually matches your buyers, your market, and your operational reality.

Here's the short answer: if your customers are primarily US-based enterprises, start with SOC 2. If you're selling internationally or into European markets, ISO 27001 carries more weight. If you're scaling fast and selling into both, you'll probably need both — but start with whichever unlocks your next 10 deals.

What SOC 2 actually is

SOC 2 is an audit framework developed by the American Institute of CPAs (AICPA). It evaluates your organization against five trust service criteria: security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy. Most companies start with security only, then add criteria as customers demand them.

The key thing about SOC 2: it's an attestation report, not a certification. A CPA firm audits your controls and issues a report. Type I evaluates the design of your controls at a point in time. Type II evaluates the operating effectiveness of those controls over a period (usually 3-12 months). Type II is what serious buyers want.

SOC 2 is flexible — you define your own controls that meet the criteria. This is both a strength (you can tailor it to your environment) and a weakness (two SOC 2 reports can look very different).

What ISO 27001 actually is

ISO 27001 is an international standard for information security management systems (ISMS). Unlike SOC 2, it's a certification — an accredited certification body audits your ISMS and issues a certificate that's valid for three years (with annual surveillance audits).

ISO 27001 is more prescriptive than SOC 2. It requires you to implement an ISMS with defined policies, risk assessments, and a specific set of controls from Annex A. The 2022 revision reorganized these into 93 controls across four themes: organizational, people, physical, and technological.

ISO 27001 carries global recognition. If you're selling into Europe, the UK, Asia-Pacific, or the Middle East, this is often the first thing procurement teams ask for.

The practical differences that matter

Geography: SOC 2 dominates North America. ISO 27001 is the global standard. If your pipeline is 80% US-based, SOC 2 is the faster path to closing deals. If you have significant international revenue, ISO 27001 unlocks more doors.

Buyer expectations: US enterprise buyers (especially in financial services, healthcare, and tech) typically ask for SOC 2 Type II reports. European and APAC buyers ask for ISO 27001 certificates. Government buyers may want neither — they'll ask for NIST 800-53, FedRAMP, or CMMC instead.

Cost: Both frameworks cost real money. SOC 2 Type II audits typically run $15,000-$50,000 depending on scope and auditor. ISO 27001 certification audits are in a similar range, but you also need a formal ISMS — which means more upfront documentation work. Budget $20,000-$60,000 for your first year of either, including tooling and audit fees.

Timeline: Most companies can achieve SOC 2 Type I in 2-4 months and Type II in 6-12 months (the observation period is the bottleneck). ISO 27001 certification typically takes 6-12 months depending on your starting posture.

Overlap: About 70% of the control work overlaps. If you build your security program well, adding the second framework after the first is significantly cheaper and faster. This is where cross-framework control mapping (something Kabrios automates) pays for itself.

When to pursue both

Most growing SaaS companies end up with both frameworks within 18-24 months. The trigger is usually a deal that requires the framework you don't have. Rather than scrambling reactively, plan for both and build a control set that satisfies both from the start.

The control overlap means the second framework is typically 30-40% less work than the first if you plan ahead. If you don't plan ahead, you're rebuilding from scratch — which is exactly the spreadsheet-stitching problem most compliance teams complain about.
